My Mental Practice Guide

Chapter 3 Motor Imagery

What is Motor Imagery?

Motor imagery is a form of therapy that can be used to strengthen the arms, hands and legs which may be weakened by stroke. In motor imagery, we mentally rehearse the movement of the affected body parts, without ever actually attempting to perform the movement. In other words, you imagine doing the motion in your mind. For example, you may imagine drinking a cup of tea, focusing on both ‘seeing’ and ‘feeling’ your hand and arm movements.

How do I do motor imagery?

You will imagine yourself undertaking the movement without actually doing the movement. You should be consciously aware of what you feel, see and hear during the imagined movements. You can choose the meaningful activities you want to improve. For example, you might want to practice the activity ‘drinking from a cup’ with the affected arm. First, you imagine you are sitting upright in front of the table. Your hands are lying on your lap. Before grasping the cup, you must imagine the feeling and effort involved as if you were lifting your hand and straightening your arm. Imagine you are controlling the movements of your hand. Imagine extending your fingers and opening your hand. When your fingers grasp the cup, focusing on ‘feeling’ your hand movements. How does it feel to grasp the cup? Is the cup warm or cold? How does the shape and texture of the cup feel? You lift the cup, being careful to keep it in balance. Imagine your elbow bending as you bring the brim of the cup to touch your lip. You can taste the juice on your tongue, and then you imagine swallowing. The next step is to tilt the cup back and straighten your elbow. You place the cup back on the table. Imagine the feeling and effort of opening your hand and letting go of the cup. Return your hand to your lap.
